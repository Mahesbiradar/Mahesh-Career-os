# Job Preparation Skill Tree — Mahesh Career OS

> **Goal:** Build a professional presence and preparation system to maximize hiring success within the target window.
> **Target Roles:** Full Stack Developer, Python Developer, Backend Developer, Cloud Engineer, Data Engineer.
> **Target Salary:** 6-8 LPA minimum.
> **Context:** Career changer with 8+ years of domain experience, real projects, and strong ownership mentality.

---

## The Job Preparation System Overview

Job preparation is not a single activity — it is a system with several interdependent components:

```
Job Preparation System
│
├── Identity Assets (Resume, LinkedIn, GitHub, Portfolio)
│   └── These tell your story when you're not in the room
│
├── Interview Readiness (Technical, Behavioral, System Design)
│   └── These determine whether you pass when you are in the room
│
├── Application Engine (Job Search, Applications, Tracking)
│   └── These determine how many rooms you get into
│
└── Network & Pipeline (Referrals, Community, Personal Brand)
    └── These multiply every other component
```

## Module 1 — Resume

---

### 1.1 Resume Strategy

- **Why It Matters:** Your resume has 6-10 seconds to pass the initial screen. The strategy determines whether it gets read.
- **Prerequisites:** All technical skills
- **Interview Importance:** N/A (pre-interview gate)
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:

**Resume Goals:**
- Pass ATS (Applicant Tracking System) filtering
- Earn human attention in 6 seconds
- Get the interview

**Mahesh's Resume Strategy:**
- Lead with projects (strong evidence of capability, not just credentials)
- Highlight career transition as an asset (domain expertise + technical skills)
- Use the exact keywords from job descriptions (ATS optimization)
- Quantify everything (see Communication Syllabus — Quantifying Achievements)

---

### 1.2 Resume Structure

- **Why It Matters:** Structure determines what gets seen first. Hiring managers read F-pattern: top-left to right, then down the left.
- **Prerequisites:** 1.1
- **Interview Importance:** N/A
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:

**Recommended Section Order (for career changers with projects):**
1. Header (name, email, phone, LinkedIn, GitHub, portfolio URL)
2. Summary/Objective (3-4 lines — who you are, what you bring)
3. Skills (technical skills table — languages, frameworks, tools, databases)
4. Projects (3-5 highlighted projects with metrics)
5. Education
6. Experience (prior telecom experience, reframed)
7. Certifications (if any)

**Why Projects before Experience:**
- Experience is 8 years in a different domain
- Projects are direct evidence of software capability
- Interviewers at startups care more about projects than credentials

---

### 1.3 Resume Writing — Section-by-Section

- **Why It Matters:** The quality of writing in each section directly impacts callbacks.
- **Prerequisites:** 1.2
- **Interview Importance:** N/A
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:

**Header:**
- Full name (prominent)
- Professional email (firstname.lastname@gmail.com)
- Phone number
- City (Bangalore, India — or just "Bangalore")
- LinkedIn URL (customized: linkedin.com/in/mahesh-biradar)
- GitHub URL
- Portfolio URL (if exists)

**Summary Statement:**
- 3-4 sentences maximum
- Template: "Software engineer transitioning from [X] with [N] years of [domain] experience. Built [projects]. Skilled in [tech stack]. Looking for [role type] where I can [contribution]."
- Keywords: Python, Django, REST API, React, PostgreSQL, Full Stack

**Skills Section:**
- Organized by category (Languages, Frameworks, Databases, Tools, Cloud)
- Only list skills you can be interviewed on
- Proficiency levels (optional — if you use them, be honest)

**Projects Section (most important):**
- Project name + brief description (one line)
- Tech stack used (bold or bullet)
- 2-3 bullet points: what you built, what problem it solved, metrics
- GitHub link + Live demo link (if available)
- Structure per bullet: strong action verb + what + result/metric

**Experience Section:**
- Job title, company, dates, location
- 3-5 bullet points per role
- Reframe telecom experience:
  - Project management → "Led cross-functional team of 5, delivered network rollout ahead of schedule"
  - Business analysis → "Analyzed data from 50+ client sites to optimize resource allocation"
  - Technical work → "Configured and maintained network infrastructure serving 10,000+ subscribers"
- Transferable skills: system design, client communication, process optimization

### 1.4 ATS Optimization

- **Why It Matters:** Most companies use ATS to filter resumes before human review. Non-ATS-friendly resumes never get read.
- **Prerequisites:** 1.3
- **Interview Importance:** N/A
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:
- Use plain formatting (no tables, no columns, no images for ATS submission)
- Standard section headings ("Experience" not "Professional Journey")
- Keywords from job description: match exact phrases
- File format: PDF (preserves formatting for human review) — but check job posting instructions
- Font: standard fonts (Arial, Calibri, Times New Roman)
- No headers/footers in ATS submission (text may not parse)
- Test with free ATS checkers (Jobscan, ResumeWorded)

---

### 1.5 One-Page Rule

- **Why It Matters:** Indian companies and most startups expect one-page resumes for candidates with under 10 years of experience.
- **Prerequisites:** 1.3
- **Interview Importance:** N/A
- **Job Importance:** High

Topics:
- Keep to one page (strictly)
- Techniques for fitting:
  - Reduce margins (0.5 inch minimum)
  - 10-11pt font (not smaller)
  - Compress prior experience (1-2 bullets max per old role)
  - Remove old/irrelevant positions
  - One-line project descriptions
- The second page rule: only acceptable for 10+ years of relevant experience

---

### 1.6 Resume Tailoring

- **Why It Matters:** A generic resume gets generic results. Tailored resumes get callbacks.
- **Prerequisites:** 1.3, 1.4
- **Interview Importance:** N/A
- **Job Importance:** High

Topics:
- Keep a "master resume" with everything
- Create tailored versions per role type:
  - Full Stack version (emphasize React + Django + PostgreSQL)
  - Backend version (emphasize Django, DRF, databases, API design)
  - Python Developer version (emphasize Python depth + data handling)
  - Cloud/DevOps version (emphasize AWS, Docker, CI/CD)
- Keyword matching: identify top 5-10 keywords in job description, ensure they appear in resume
- Mirror the company's language (enterprise vs startup tone)

## Module 2 — LinkedIn

---

### 2.1 LinkedIn Profile Optimization

- **Why It Matters:** Recruiters primarily use LinkedIn. A weak profile = missed opportunities.
- **Prerequisites:** 1.3
- **Interview Importance:** N/A
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:

**Profile Photo:**
- Professional, clear headshot
- Light background, face visible
- Smile — approachable
- No sunglasses, no group photos

**Background Banner:**
- Custom banner (Canva) with tech stack logos or location
- Simple and professional

**Headline:**
- Not just your job title
- Formula: "What you do | What you want to do | Key skills"
- Example: "Software Engineer | Python · Django · React | Building Full Stack Applications"
- Keywords here are indexed by LinkedIn search

**About Section:**
- 3-5 paragraphs
- Paragraph 1: Who you are and what you're building toward
- Paragraph 2: Your projects and what you've built
- Paragraph 3: Your background and what it brings to software
- Paragraph 4: What you're looking for
- End with: your email and LinkedIn profile URL for easy contact
- Include keywords naturally

**Experience Section:**
- Match LinkedIn experience to resume
- Current: "Building software projects, learning Python/Django/React full stack"
- Past telecom experience: reframed with transferable skills

- **Prerequisites:** 2.1

### 2.4 LinkedIn Networking & Outreach

- **Why It Matters:** 60-70% of jobs are filled through networks, not applications. Outreach creates opportunities before they're posted.
- **Prerequisites:** 2.3
- **Interview Importance:** N/A
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:

**Connection Strategy:**
- Connect with: developers at target companies, hiring managers, recruiters, bootcamp grads, fellow learners
- Always add a personalized connection note (never send blank request)
- Target: 5 new relevant connections per week

**Cold Outreach Message Template:**
```
Hi [Name],

I came across your profile and noticed you're working as a [role] at [company]. 
I'm transitioning into software engineering with a background in telecom, 
and I've been building projects in [stack]. I'd love to learn about your 
experience and any advice you have for someone breaking into [industry/role].

Would you be open to a 15-minute conversation?

Best,
Mahesh
```

**Warm Outreach (for referrals):**
```
Hi [Name],

I saw [Company] is hiring for [Role]. I've been building projects in 
[stack], including [specific relevant project]. I believe I'd be a strong 
fit because [specific reason].

Would you be willing to refer me or let me know who I should speak with?
I'd really appreciate it.

Best,
Mahesh
```

**Informational Interview:**
- Ask for 15-20 minute calls
- Questions: how did you get into this role? What does day-to-day look like? Advice for career changers?
- NOT: do you have a job for me?

## Module 3 — GitHub Portfolio

---

### 3.1 Repository Organization

- **Why It Matters:** Recruiters and interviewers look at GitHub. Messy repos = negative signal.
- **Prerequisites:** Git & GitHub knowledge
- **Interview Importance:** Medium-High
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:
- Delete or archive unused/experiment repos
- Naming conventions: `project-name` (lowercase, hyphens)
- README for every pinned repository
- Consistent profile (public username, professional photo, bio, website, location)
- Organization accounts for project groupings (optional)

---

### 3.2 Pinned Repositories

- **Why It Matters:** Pinned repos are the first 6 things anyone sees. Choose them deliberately.
- **Prerequisites:** 3.1
- **Interview Importance:** High
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:
- Pin 4-6 repositories
- Prioritize: best projects, most complete, most impressive tech
- Include: at least one full-stack project, one backend project, one with tests
- Variety: different problem domains (show breadth)
- One project per target domain (web app, API, data, etc.)

---

### 3.3 README Quality

- **Why It Matters:** A good README is the difference between someone exploring your project and bouncing.
- **Prerequisites:** 3.1
- **Interview Importance:** High
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:

**README Structure:**
1. Project title + brief description (one sentence: "A tool to manage library inventory...")
2. Demo screenshot or GIF (visual = immediate engagement)
3. Live demo link (if deployed)
4. Tech stack (bullet list or badges)
5. Features list
6. Setup instructions (local development)
7. API documentation (for backend projects)
8. Architecture overview (optional but impressive)
9. What I learned / challenges (shows reflection)

**README Quality Standards:**
- No typos or grammatical errors
- Code blocks for all commands
- Working instructions (test them yourself!)
- Badges (build status, coverage, version)
- No "TODO: add README later" placeholders

---

### 3.4 Contribution Graph

- **Why It Matters:** The green squares signal consistency and dedication.
- **Prerequisites:** 3.1
- **Interview Importance:** Medium
- **Job Importance:** Medium-High

Topics:
- Daily commits build the graph
- Commit types that count: code, README updates, documentation
- Quality over quantity: meaningful commits, not trivial ones
- Consistent activity > burst activity (show discipline)
- Working on public repos (private commits don't show on public graph)
- Don't game it (empty commits, automated noise) — it's obvious

---

### 3.5 Open Source Contributions (Advanced)

- **Why It Matters:** Open source contributions signal professionalism and collaboration ability.
- **Prerequisites:** 3.4
- **Interview Importance:** High (when present)
- **Job Importance:** Medium-High

Topics:
- Start small: fix typos in docs, improve README
- First contributions: look for "good first issue" labels
- Target projects: Django, DRF, projects you use in your own work
- PR quality: clear description, small scope, follow contribution guidelines
- Maintaining your own projects: respond to issues, welcome PRs
- GitHub Discussions participation

## Module 5 — Interview Preparation System

---

### 5.1 Interview Types

- **Why It Matters:** Different interview types require different preparation. Confusing them wastes prep time.
- **Prerequisites:** Communication Syllabus
- **Interview Importance:** Critical
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:

**Screening Round (30-45 min):**
- Usually with HR or recruiter
- Resume walkthrough
- Basic technical screening
- Salary expectation discussion
- Culture fit

**Technical Round 1 (60 min):**
- Core CS concepts (OS, Networks, DBMS)
- Language fundamentals (Python OOP, etc.)
- SQL queries
- Sometimes DSA

**Technical Round 2 (60 min):**
- Framework-specific (Django, DRF)
- System design (junior level: explain your project architecture)
- Code writing / debugging
- Deeper CS theory

**Final/Leadership Round (45 min):**
- Behavioral questions
- "Tell me about yourself" in depth
- Career goals
- Cultural fit
- Salary negotiation

---

### 5.2 Interview Question Bank

- **Why It Matters:** Preparation is the only way to perform well. A question bank structures preparation.
- **Prerequisites:** All technical syllabi
- **Interview Importance:** Critical
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:

**Python Questions (Common):**
- What are Python's data types?
- Difference between list and tuple
- Mutable vs immutable objects
- How does Python's GIL work?
- What are decorators?
- What is a generator? How is it different from a function?
- What is the difference between `@classmethod` and `@staticmethod`?
- What is method resolution order (MRO)?
- How does memory management work in Python?
- What is a context manager?

**Django Questions (Common):**
- What is the Django MVT pattern?
- What is the difference between `ForeignKey`, `OneToOneField`, and `ManyToManyField`?
- How do you optimize Django queries?
- What is N+1 problem and how do you solve it?
- What is Django middleware?
- How does Django authentication work?
- What are Django signals?
- How does Django's ORM work?

**DRF Questions (Common):**
- What is the difference between Serializer and ModelSerializer?
- How do you handle authentication in DRF?
- What is the difference between APIView, GenericView, and ViewSet?
- How do you implement custom permissions?
- How do you implement pagination?

**SQL Questions (Common):**
- Difference between INNER JOIN and LEFT JOIN
- What is a window function?
- What is an index and when would you not use one?
- What is the difference between DELETE, DROP, and TRUNCATE?
- Write a query to find the Nth highest salary
- What is a CTE?
- What are ACID properties?
- What is a transaction?

**OS Questions (Common):**
- What is a process vs a thread?
- What is a deadlock and how do you prevent it?
- What is virtual memory?
- What is a context switch?
- What is the difference between mutex and semaphore?
- What are ACID properties?

**Networks Questions (Common):**
- What happens when you type a URL in a browser?
- What is the difference between TCP and UDP?
- What is HTTPS and how does TLS work?
- What is a REST API?
- What is the difference between authentication and authorization?
- What are HTTP status codes?
- What is CORS?

**Behavioral Questions:**
(see Communication Syllabus, Level 5.5)

- **Why It Matters:** Junior/mid-level companies ask about your project architecture, not full system design. Be prepared to discuss yours.
- **Prerequisites:** All technical syllabi
- **Interview Importance:** High
- **Job Importance:** High

Topics:

**Your Project Architecture (for each project):**

- What technologies did you use and why?
- How does the authentication work?
- How is data stored and retrieved?

- What are the current limitations?

### 5.4 Company Research Framework

- **Why It Matters:** Interviewers can tell when you've done research. It signals interest and seriousness.
- **Prerequisites:** 5.1
- **Interview Importance:** High
- **Job Importance:** High

Topics:

**Research Checklist (before every interview):**
- What does the company do? (business model)
- What is their tech stack? (LinkedIn, job posts, engineering blog)
- What is their growth stage? (startup, mid-size, enterprise)
- What are their current challenges? (news, product reviews)
- Who are their main competitors?
- What does the engineering culture look like? (Glassdoor, Blind, engineering blog)
- Who is the interviewer? (LinkedIn research)
- What are their values? (careers page)

**Use research in interview:**
- "I noticed your engineering blog mentions that you're migrating to X..."
- "I saw that you recently launched Y feature — I was curious about the technical challenges..."
- "Based on what I read, your team uses Django, which aligns with what I've been building..."

## Module 6 — Application Engine

---

### 6.1 Job Search Strategy

- **Why It Matters:** Random applications to random jobs = low conversion. Targeted strategy = efficient use of time.
- **Prerequisites:** All modules
- **Interview Importance:** N/A
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:

**Target Company Tiers:**
- Tier 1: Dream companies (high-bar, prepare extensively)
- Tier 2: Strong-fit companies (right-size challenge)
- Tier 3: Practice companies (good for interview practice early on)

**Job Boards (India):**
- LinkedIn Jobs (primary)
- Naukri.com (volume, good for service companies)
- AngelList/Wellfound (startups)
- Instahyre (quality filter)
- HackerNews "Who's Hiring" (remote + international)
- Company careers pages directly

**Search Strategy:**
- Keywords: "Python Developer", "Django Developer", "Full Stack Developer", "Backend Engineer Python"
- Location: Bangalore (in-person) + Remote
- Company size filter: start with 50-500 employees (faster processes, more impact)
- Date posted: "Past week" (avoid stale listings)

---

### 6.2 Application Quality

- **Why It Matters:** Quality over quantity. 10 tailored applications > 100 generic ones.
- **Prerequisites:** Module 1
- **Interview Importance:** N/A
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:

**Before Applying Checklist:**
- Resume tailored with JD keywords
- Cover letter (or LinkedIn message) if personal connection possible
- Portfolio/GitHub ready to share
- LinkedIn profile updated

**Cover Letter Template (if required):**
- Paragraph 1: Why this company specifically (research)
- Paragraph 2: What you bring (your strongest qualification)
- Paragraph 3: Your most relevant project (specific)
- Paragraph 4: Ask for the interview

**Application Volume:**
- Aim for 5-10 quality applications per week
- Track every application (see 6.3)

---

### 6.3 Application Tracking System

- **Why It Matters:** Following up on the right applications at the right time requires tracking. Scattered applications lose opportunities.
- **Prerequisites:** 6.2
- **Interview Importance:** N/A
- **Job Importance:** High

Topics:

**Tracking Spreadsheet Columns:**
- Company
- Role
- Date Applied
- Source (LinkedIn, Naukri, Referral)
- Status (Applied, Screening, Technical 1, Technical 2, Final, Offer, Rejected)
- Next Action
- Notes
- Contact name
- Salary range

**Follow-up Protocol:**
- After application: wait 5-7 business days, then LinkedIn message to recruiter/hiring manager
- After interview: email thank-you within 24 hours
- After silence (1 week post-interview): follow up once
- After rejection: thank them, ask for feedback (some will give it), stay connected

---

### 6.4 Salary Negotiation

- **Why It Matters:** Most candidates accept the first offer. Negotiation is expected and professional.
- **Prerequisites:** 6.3
- **Interview Importance:** N/A
- **Job Importance:** Critical (financial impact compounds over career)

Topics:

**Research:**
- Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, AmbitionBox for salary data
- LinkedIn salary insights
- Ask network contacts confidentially
- Know your target: 6-8 LPA minimum

**Negotiation Principles:**
- Never give a number first if possible ("I'm flexible and open to discussion based on the role")
- If you must give a range: give your target as the floor, not the ceiling
- Silence is powerful (after they give a number, pause before responding)
- Counteroffers: "Thank you for the offer. Based on my research and the skills I bring, I was expecting X. Is there flexibility?"
- Consider total compensation: base + bonus + benefits + growth potential

**Mahesh's Positioning:**
- 8 years of domain expertise = rare combination
- Real projects = demonstrated ability (not just theoretical)
- Career changer urgency = motivated, will ramp quickly
- Minimum floor: 6 LPA; target: 8 LPA; stretch: 10 LPA

### 7.3 Referral Strategy

- **Why It Matters:** Referred candidates are 4x more likely to be interviewed and 2x more likely to be hired.
- **Prerequisites:** 7.1
- **Interview Importance:** N/A
- **Job Importance:** Critical

Topics:

**The Referral Hierarchy:**
1. Direct friend at the company (highest conversion)
2. Online friend/connection who works there
3. Alumni from your institute who works there (reach via LinkedIn)
4. Cold connection at the company (still better than no referral)

**Referral Request Protocol:**
- Research: find target company → find employees → check connections
- Warm up: engage with their LinkedIn content before asking
- Be specific: ask for referral for a specific role (have the JD URL ready)
- Make it easy: send your resume, a 2-sentence pitch, and the job link
- Be gracious: thank them regardless of outcome

**Who to ask:**
- Former colleagues and classmates
- Connections from LinkedIn (after relationship building)
- Bootcamp/course cohort members
- Tech community members
